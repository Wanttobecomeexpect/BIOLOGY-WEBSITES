Animal Kingdom

Introduction to Animal Kingdom

The Animal Kingdom (Kingdom Animalia) includes all multicellular, eukaryotic, heterotrophic organisms. Animals cannot prepare their own food and thus depend on other organisms. They show great diversity in structure, habitat, and functions.

Key Concept

Animals are classified into various groups based on their characteristics and evolutionary history.

Animal Kingdom

The cell theory is one of the fundamental principles of biology. It consists of three main components:

  1. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and organization in organisms.
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

Bases of Classification

Prokaryotic cells are simpler and smaller than eukaryotic cells. They lack a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. Bacteria and archaea are prokaryotic organisms.

Porifera

General Characteristics
  1. Habitat: All are aquatic. (mostly marine)
  2. Level of Organization: Cellular level of organization.
  3. Symmetry: Asymmetrical.
  4. Germ layer: Diploblastic (two germ layers).
  5. Body plan: Cellular aggregation with a porous body.
  6. Coelom: Acoelomate (no body cavity).
  7. Metamerism: Absent.
  8. Notochord: Absent.
Physiology
  1. Digestive System: Intracellular digestion.
  2. Respiratory System: Absent
  3. Circulatory System: Absent
  4. Excretory System: Absent
  5. Reproduction: Asexual (by Fragmentation) and sexual reproduction.
  6. Fertilisation: Internal
  7. Development: Indirect (via larval stage)

Notes

1. These are SESSILE animals which means they are attached by Ocean floor

2. The body is supported by SKELETON made of spongin fibres, SPICULES (Calcium or silicon)

Examples

1. Sycon (a type of sponge)

2. Spongilla (freshwater sponge)

Annelida

General Characteristics
  1. Habitat: Marine, freshwater, and terrestrial environments.
  2. Level of Organization: Organ system level of organization.
  3. Symmetry: Bilateral symmetry.
  4. Germ layer: Triploblastic (three germ layers).
  5. Body plan: Segmented body plan.
  6. Coelom: Coelomate (true body cavity lined with mesoderm).
  7. Metamerism: Present.
  8. Notochord: Absent.
Physiology
  1. Digestive System: Complete digestive system (mouth and anus).
  2. Respiratory System: Gills or lungs (depending on habitat).
  3. Circulatory System: Closed circulatory system (blood vessels present).
  4. Excretory System: Nephridia (excretory organs) present.
  5. Reproduction: Asexual (by budding) and sexual reproduction.
  6. Fertilisation: External.
  7. Development: Indirect (via larval stage).

Hemichordata

General Characteristics
  1. Habitat: Marine environments (mostly).
  2. Level of Organization: Organ system level of organization.
  3. Symmetry: Bilateral symmetry.
  4. Germ layer: Triploblastic (three germ layers).
  5. Body plan: Segmented body plan.
  6. Coelom: Coelomate (true body cavity lined with mesoderm).
  7. Metamerism: Present (segmentation evident).
  8. Notochord: Present in larval stage, absent in adults.
Physiology
  1. Digestive System: Complete digestive system (mouth and anus).
  2. Respiratory System: Gills or lungs (depending on habitat).
  3. Circulatory System: Open circulatory system (blood not always contained in vessels).
  4. Excretory System: Nephridia (excretory organs) present.
  5. Reproduction: Sexual reproduction (dioecious).
  6. Fertilisation: External fertilization (in water).
  7. Development: Indirect development (larval stage present).

Chordata

General Characteristics
  1. Habitat: Aquatic and terrestrial.
  2. Level of Organization: Organ system level.
  3. Symmetry: Bilateral symmetry.
  4. Germ layer: Triploblastic (three germ layers).
  5. Body plan: Coelomate (true body cavity).
  6. Coelom: Present (body cavity lined with mesoderm).
  7. Metamerism: Present (segmentation evident).
  8. Notochord: Present (embryonic structure).
Physiology
  1. Digestive System: Complete digestive tract (mouth and anus).
  2. Respiratory System: Gills (in aquatic forms) or lungs (in terrestrial forms).
  3. Circulatory System: Closed circulatory system (blood contained within vessels).
  4. Excretory System: Kidneys (for osmoregulation and waste removal).
  5. Reproduction: Sexual reproduction (most species).
  6. Fertilisation: Internal or external fertilization (depending on species).
  7. Development: Embryonic development (with distinct stages).
