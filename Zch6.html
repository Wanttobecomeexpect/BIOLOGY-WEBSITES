The Living World

Introduction

The term “living” refers to organisms that show growth, reproduction, metabolism, and response to stimuli.

Basic Characteristics of Living Organisms

  • Cellular organization
  • Growth and development
  • Ability to reproduce
  • Metabolism (biochemical reactions)
  • Response to the environment
  • Adaptation and evolution

Study of Living Organisms

To study the vast diversity of life, biologists classify organisms into groups based on similarities and differences.

Taxonomy is the science of classification of organisms, while systematics deals with taxonomy along with evolutionary relationships.

Nomenclature (naming) of organisms follows rules given by ICZN (for animals) and ICBN (for plants).

Diversity in Living World

Biodiversity refers to the variety and variability of living organisms present on Earth, including plants, animals, microorganisms, and their ecosystems.

Types of Biodiversity

Species Diversity: Different species like plants, animals, microbes

Genetic Diversity: Variations within a species, e.g. rice varieties, dog breeds

Ecosystem Diversity: Different ecosystems: forests, deserts, oceans, wetlands

About 1.7 – 1.8 million species are described so far.

Scientists estimate there may be over 10 million species on Earth.

Biodiversity is crucial for ecological balance, food, medicines, and raw materials.

It ensures ecosystem stability and supports evolution.

Nomenclature

Biological nomenclature is the system of providing scientific names to living organisms. This standardized method was introduced by Carl Linnaeus using the system of Binomial Nomenclature.

Key Features of Binomial Nomenclature

  • Each organism has two names:
    → Generic name (Genus), starts with a capital letter.
    → Specific epithet (Species), starts with a small letter.
  • Names are universal (same worldwide).
  • Scientific names are in Latin or derived from Latin.
  • When printed → italicized; when handwritten → underlined.

Why is Classification is Important

  1. Organises diversity: Groups millions of organisms for easy study.
  2. Reveals relationships: Helps understand similarities, differences, and evolution.
  3. Avoids confusion: Uses a uniform, standard system across the world.
  4. Predictive value: Knowing the group helps predict traits of its members.
  5. Practical uses: Supports agriculture, medicine, forestry, and conservation.

In short: classification is the backbone for systematically studying Earth’s biodiversity.

Taxa

  • The categories or levels in the classification system are called taxa (singular: taxon).
  • Example of Taxa (from broad to narrow):
    • Kingdom → Phylum/Division → Class → Order → Family → Genus → Species

Taxonomy Categories

Taxonomy categories are the hierarchical levels used to classify and organize living organisms.

Taxanomic Hierarchy

The taxonomic hierarchy is the systematic arrangement of living organisms into successive levels or ranks based on similarities and differences. It was introduced by Carl Linnaeus and forms the backbone of classification.

Example:
  • Kingdom: Animalia
  • Phylum: Chordata
  • Class: Mammalia
  • Order: Primates
  • Family: Hominidae
  • Genus: Homo
  • Species: Homo sapiens

Taxon

A taxon (plural: taxa) is a unit or level in the taxonomic hierarchy. Each rank such as kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, or species is called a taxon.

Kingdom

  1. Difinition:The highest taxonomic rank. It includes a very large group of organisms that share only a few common features.
  2. Example:
    Animalia: all multicellular, heterotrophic animals
    Plantae: all green plants

Phylum (in animal)/ Division (in plants)

  1. Difinition:The second level below kingdom. Organisms are grouped into phyla/divisions based on fundamental body plans or structures.
  2. Example:
    Phylum Chordata (animals with notochord)
    Division Angiosperms (plants with flowers & seeds in fruits)

Order

  1. Difinition:A subdivision of a phylum. Members share more similarities than at the phylum level./li>
  2. Example:
    Class Mammalia (all mammals → warm-blooded, hair, mammary glands)
    Class Dicotyledonae (plants with two seed leaves)

Family

  1. Difinition:A group of closely related genera. More specific than order.
  2. Example:
    Family Hominidae (humans and great apes)
    Family Fabaceae (pea, beans, gram plants)

Genus

  1. Difinition:A group of closely related species that have common characteristics.
  2. Example:
    Homo: (includes humans, extinct relatives)
    Solanum: (includes potato, tomato, brinjal)

Species

  1. Difinition:The basic unit of classification. A group of organisms that can interbreed and produce fertile offspring.
  2. Example:
    Homo sapiens (modern humans)
    Panthera leo (lion)

Notes

Kingdom→ broaddest group

Species→ smallest, most specific group

Each step down the hierarchy → organisms share more similarities

Chapter Summary

The living world is highly diverse, consisting of millions of species of plants, animals, and microorganisms. Living organisms show common characteristics such as growth, reproduction, metabolism, response to stimuli, cellular organization, and the ability to adapt and evolve. To study this vast diversity, classification and naming of organisms are essential. Scientific naming follows binomial nomenclature (Genus + Species) introduced by Linnaeus. The systematic arrangement of organisms into groups based on similarities and differences is called taxonomy, while systematics also considers evolutionary relationships. The taxonomic hierarchy includes ranks like kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species. Various taxonomic aids like herbaria, museums, botanical gardens, zoological parks, and keys help in identification and classification of organisms.
