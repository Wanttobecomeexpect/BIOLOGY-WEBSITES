Aestivation

Aestivation refers to the arrangement of floral parts in a flower bud before it opens. It is an important characteristic used in the classification of plants. There are several types of aestivation, including valvate, imbricate, twisted, vexillary.

Types of Aestivation

  • Valvate: Sepals or petals are arranged in a bud without overlapping.
  • Imbricate: Sepals or petals overlap each other like roof tiles.
  • Twisted: Sepals or petals are twisted in a spiral arrangement.
  • Vexillary: One petal is larger and encloses the others like a banner.

Placentation

Placentation refers to the arrangement of ovules within the ovary of a flower. It is an important characteristic used in the classification of plants. There are several types of placentation, including marginal, axile, parietal, free central, and basal.

Types of Placentation

  • Marginal: Ovules are arranged along the margin of a single carpel.
  • Axile: Ovules are arranged in a central column within a multi-carpel ovary.
  • Parietal: Ovules are attached to the inner wall of a multi-carpel ovary.
  • Free Central: Ovules are attached to a central column without any septa.
  • Basal: Ovules are attached at the base of the ovary.

Fruits

Fruits are the mature ovaries of flowering plants, usually containing seeds. They play a crucial role in the reproduction and dispersal of plants. Fruits can be classified into various types based on their structure, development, and the number of ovaries involved.

Types of Fruits

  • Simple Fruits: Develop from a single ovary of a single flower. Examples include cherries, peaches, and tomatoes.
  • Aggregate Fruits: Form from multiple ovaries of a single flower. Examples include raspberries and blackberries.
  • Multiple Fruits: Develop from the ovaries of multiple flowers growing in a cluster. Examples include pineapples and figs.

Seeds

Seeds are the reproductive structures in flowering plants that contain the embryo and the necessary nutrients for its development. They are formed from the fertilized ovule and play a crucial role in the plant's life cycle. Seeds can vary greatly in size, shape, and structure, depending on the species.

Structure of Seeds

A typical seed consists of three main parts:

  • Seed Coat: The outer protective layer that surrounds the seed.
  • Embryo: The young plant that develops from the fertilized ovule.
  • Endosperm: The tissue that provides nourishment to the developing embryo.

Chapter Summary

The living world is highly diverse, consisting of millions of species of plants, animals, and microorganisms. Living organisms show common characteristics such as growth, reproduction, metabolism, response to stimuli, cellular organization, and the ability to adapt and evolve. To study this vast diversity, classification and naming of organisms are essential. Scientific naming follows binomial nomenclature (Genus + Species) introduced by Linnaeus. The systematic arrangement of organisms into groups based on similarities and differences is called taxonomy, while systematics also considers evolutionary relationships. The taxonomic hierarchy includes ranks like kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species. Various taxonomic aids like herbaria, museums, botanical gardens, zoological parks, and keys help in identification and classification of organisms.
