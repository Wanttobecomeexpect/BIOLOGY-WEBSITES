Biological Classification

Introduction

Schemes of Classification

Earliest Classification by 'Early Men':

It was non-scientific classification based on 'uses' of organisms.

Example:
  • Food: crops, wheat, vegetables etc
  • Shelter: teak, oak wook etc
  • Clothing: silk, cotten, wool etc
  • Security: dogs
  • Transport: horse, bull, mule etc
  • Other: medicine, gums, resins etc

First scientific classification:

It was proposed by Aristotle (384-322 BC) who classified organisms into two groups:

  • Plants: Based on simple Morphology characteristics (like size and structure) into herbs, shrubs, trees
  • Animals: Based on presence of red blood cell and white blood cell into Enaima (red blooded animals) and Anaima (white blooded animals)

Two kingdom classification

It is given by Carolus Linnaeus and it's main character are presence and absence of cell wall

Example:
  • Plants (cell wall present): Archaebacteria, Eubacteria, Chrysophytes, Dinoflagellates, Fungi, Algae, Pteridophytes, Gymnosperms, Angiosperms.
  • Animals (cell wall absent): Protozoa, insects, mammals, etc.
Drawbacks:
  • This system did not distinguished between prokaryotic (bacteria, BGA), and eukaryotic (fungi, plants) organisms.
  • It kept together unicellular (chlorella) with multicellular plants.
  • It kept heterotrophic (fungi) and autotrophic (plants) organism together.

Note:

Some organism like mycoplasma lacked cell wall but kept with bacteria in plants.

5-Kingdom system

It given by R.H. Whittaker in 1969 and he introduced Kingdom Fungi.

Main criteria were:

  1. Cell structure: Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic
  2. Body organization: Cellular, multicellular, loose tissue, tissue, organ, organ system.
  3. Mode of Nutrition: Autotrophic and Heterotrophic
  4. Reproduction: Asexual and Sexual
  5. Phylogentic relationships: Evolutionary relationships.

More in Protozoans

Protozoans are divided into four major groups:

  • Amoeboid Protozoans: Found in freshwater, seawater, or moist soil. They move and capture food with the help of pseudopodia. Examples: Amoeba (free-living), Entamoeba (parasite).
  • Flagellated Protozoans: Move with the help of flagella. They may be free-living or parasitic. Example: Trypanosoma (causes sleeping sickness).
  • Ciliated Protozoans: Aquatic and actively moving with the help of cilia. They have a gullet for food intake. Example: Paramecium.
  • Sporozoans: All are endoparasites and have an infectious spore-like stage in their life cycle. Example: Plasmodium (malarial parasite).
  • Because of cell wall of Chrysophytes are made up of Silica, they are indestructible.So, these cell wall accumulated for billion of years and form 'Diatomaceous earth'. this is used as polishing, filtaring oil os syrups
  • In Dinoflagellates, when red dinoflagellates goes under rapid multipication they make sea appear red but due to they are toxic, in such a large number they may even kill marinecreatures around them like fishes.

Kingdom Fungi

Characteristics of Kingdom Fungi (NCERT Class 11)

  • Eukaryotic & Heterotrophic: Fungi are eukaryotic organisms, mostly heterotrophic (saprophytic, parasitic, or symbiotic).
  • Cell Wall: Their cell wall is made of chitin and polysaccharides.
  • Body Structure: The body consists of thread-like filaments called hyphae. A network of hyphae forms the mycelium. Hyphae may be septate or coenocytic.
  • Nutrition:
    • Saprophytic – decomposers.
    • Parasitic – living on hosts.
    • Symbiotic – with algae in lichens, with roots in mycorrhiza.
  • Life Cycle:
    • Plasmogamy – fusion of protoplasm of two cells.
    • Karyogamy – fusion of nuclei.
    • Meiosis – occurs in zygote, producing haploid spores.
  • Habitats: Fungi are cosmopolitan, found in air, water, soil, decaying matter, and as parasites.
  • Classification & Examples:
    • Phycomycetes: Rhizopus, Albugo
    • Ascomycetes: Saccharomyces (yeast), Aspergillus, Neurospora
    • Basidiomycetes: Agaricus (mushroom), Puccinia
    • Deuteromycetes: Alternaria, Trichoderma

Reproduction in Fungi

Fungi can reproduce in 3 ways :

  • Vegetative reproduction – by fragmentation, fission, budding.
  • Asexual reproduction – by spores such as conidia, sporangiospores, zoospores.
  • Sexual reproduction – involves plasmogamy, karyogamy, and meiosis, producing zygospores, ascospores, or basidiospores.

    • it is done in 3 steps:

    1. Plasmogamy – fusion of protoplasm of two cells.
    2. Karyogamy – fusion of nuclei.
    3. Meiosis – occurs in zygote, producing haploid spores.

Chapter Summary

The living world is highly diverse, consisting of millions of species of plants, animals, and microorganisms. Living organisms show common characteristics such as growth, reproduction, metabolism, response to stimuli, cellular organization, and the ability to adapt and evolve. To study this vast diversity, classification and naming of organisms are essential. Scientific naming follows binomial nomenclature (Genus + Species) introduced by Linnaeus. The systematic arrangement of organisms into groups based on similarities and differences is called taxonomy, while systematics also considers evolutionary relationships. The taxonomic hierarchy includes ranks like kingdom, phylum, class, order, family, genus, and species. Various taxonomic aids like herbaria, museums, botanical gardens, zoological parks, and keys help in identification and classification of organisms.
